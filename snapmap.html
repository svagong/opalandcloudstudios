SnapMap

Description

Snap-map provides users with the tools to explore their community, via photography. Mock-ups were created in Figma, and photography was touched up in Adobe Lightroom.

iOS Prototype
Android Prototype

Objective

A native platform for photography sharing, with features for searching for locations by category and area. Tools are familiar in function to popular food and map exploration applications, including community interactive elements.

Context

Photography saves those beautiful little moments, and a good photographer knows you don't need impressive, breathtaking backgrounds to get a great shot. Snap map was made to help photographers share locations that would be great for photography, and bring the romance of the local back.

Conceptualization and User flows

Based on my own experiences with photography apps, I created three user stories to design solutions for.

  • Users can set-up a profile and receive personalized location recommendations based on their preferences.
  • Users can search for photo spots by location or type of photography.
  • Users can interact with the local photography community and leave comments and ratings on photo spots.

User flows

Writing out the user flows, the user onboards directly onto profile personalization, but always has the option to skip it and come back later. This allows any user who downloaded the app for the first time, on the go, to quickly use the app without delay.

Conclusions

Snapmap was made to help users find amazing photography spots, where ever they were. Designing the familiar map and filter features, along with photo galleries and elements for community interactions, all made for a highly intuitive and clean application. The goal of this project was to familiarize myself with native app design in both Android and iOS systems. The resulting sets of screens therefore have been tailored for each operating system, but contain the same content and usability. As a life-time Android user, I went through a couple humps trying to integrate common iOS features, such as a tab bar or top navigation element, and required user testing to tell me my use of these elements caused redundancy (as I would include both a next button and a next icon on the top and bottom of the screens) and confused users. I therefore learned a pretty beginner rule - keep things simple rather than complicated. Additional choices weren't desired, they left users unsatisfied with their final choice, and tired from having to make one.

Challenges

Designing for iOS was a challenge, as I have been an Android user my whole life. Many features that are intuitive to me as a life-time Android user were opposite what is expected for iOS. Another challenge was over-placing buttons. Users had the most to say about buttons that both ended up doing the same thing, which I completely missed while working on my initial designs.

Lessons Learned

Not to be daunted by the differences in iOS and Android and understanding that good design is fine. The two version do not need to be different for the sake of being different, and with each application I make, I also begin to understand more about user-preferences and what points cause frustrations.

Final Thoughts

I particularly enjoyed choosing and placing images for this assignment, especially the welcome screen's full screen images. As a photographer this app was exactly the type I would look for, and picking photos that suited the “local niche spots” theme was fun as I used photos from essentially my own neighborhood, and was able to explore a little on the way.
